
# Exomoon Designation: Thalassa-1b
## A Tidally Heated Ocean Moon Orbiting a Gas Giant

### 1. Overview

Thalassa-1b is a theoretical exomoon classified as a **tidally heated water world**. It orbits a Jupiter-mass gas giant within the circumstellar habitable zone of its parent star. The moon is characterized by an exceptionally deep global saltwater ocean overlying a rocky core, with a lone emergent landmass formed by persistent hotspot volcanism. Its low surface gravity and unique orbital configuration produce a pressure profile, tidal regime, and day-night cycle unlike any observed on terrestrial planets.

### 3. Internal Structure

Thalassa-1b is differentiated into two primary layers:

1.  **Rocky Core**: Radius of approximately **3,450 km**. Composed of silicates and metals. The core is geologically active, driven by radiogenic heating and, critically, tidal flexing induced by the moon's slight orbital eccentricity. The core-mantle boundary (CMB) is characterized by irregular topography, including elevated regions and deep basins, which locally thin the overlying high-pressure ice layer and facilitate direct water-rock interaction.

2.  **Global Ocean**: A continuous liquid water layer extending from the core-mantle boundary to the surface. The ocean has a uniform depth of **250 km**. This depth is defined by the hydrostatic pressure threshold for the formation of high-pressure ice phases (specifically Ice VI). Below the 250 km boundary, the pressure exceeds ~1 GPa, at which point water would solidify into a dense ice mantle. The ocean volume is approximately **120 times** the total volume of Earth's oceans.

### 4. Ocean Depth and Pressure Profile

The moon's low surface gravity (0.405 g) results in a significantly reduced hydrostatic pressure gradient compared to Earth. The pressure at any depth is given by:

`P(depth) = P_surface + ρ × g × depth`

where:
- `P_surface` = 101,325 Pa (1 atm)
- `ρ` = 1,030 kg/m³ (saltwater density)
- `g` = 3.97 m/s² (surface gravity)

This yields a pressure increase of approximately **0.0398 atm per meter** of depth, which is exactly 40% of the rate experienced on Earth (0.0994 atm/m).

**Implications of the Pressure Profile:**
- **Accessibility**: The entire 250 km water column is accessible with submersible technology requiring hull strengths only an order of magnitude greater than Earth's deepest-diving vehicles. The slow pressure gradient permits gradual compression designs and flexible pressure-equalizing structures.
- **Vertical Migration**: The pressure differential between surface and seafloor is a factor of ~10,000, but the gradient is shallow. Organisms adapted to mid-depth pressures can theoretically ascend to the surface with minimal barotrauma risk, enabling planet-wide diel vertical migration.
- **Hydrothermal Vent Stability**: At 10,000 atm seafloor pressure, the boiling point of water is elevated to approximately 600–700 K. Superheated vent fluids remain liquid over wider temperature ranges, promoting stable, long-lived chemosynthetic ecosystems.

### 5. The Emergent Landmass: Super-Hotspot Volcano

The sole subaerial feature on Thalassa-1b is a massive shield volcano formed by a persistent mantle plume originating at the core-mantle boundary. Tidal heating maintains a localized zone of partial melting, driving continuous effusive and explosive volcanism.

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Total Height** | >250 km | From seafloor base to summit above sea level |
| **Submerged Portion** | ~248 km | Majority of the volcanic edifice is underwater |
| **Subaerial Summit** | ~2 km above sea level | The only dry land on the moon |
| **Base Diameter** | ~1,200 km | Comparable to a small continent |
| **Flank Slope** | <5° | Extremely gentle, typical of low-gravity shield volcanoes |

**Volcanism Characteristics:**
- **Deep-Sea Eruptions (>100 km depth)** : Lavas are rapidly quenched by ambient pressure and cold water, forming extensive plains of hyaloclastite (volcanic glass) and pillow basalts. Explosive activity is suppressed.
- **Mid-Depth to Shallow Eruptions**: As the edifice grows upward, decreasing pressure allows for increasingly volatile-driven explosive eruptions, producing ash, pumice, and building the subaerial summit cone.
- **Hydrothermal Systems**: The entire volcanic flanks are permeated by circulating seawater, creating a vast network of hydrothermal vents. These vents discharge mineral-rich fluids, supporting a diverse chemosynthetic ecosystem and depositing massive sulfide chimneys.

### 6. Orbital Dynamics and Tidal Environment

**Rotation and Day-Night Cycle:**
Thalassa-1b is tidally locked to its parent gas giant. One hemisphere (the "sub-jovian" side) perpetually faces the planet, while the opposite hemisphere (the "anti-jovian" side) faces away. The orbital period, and thus the solar day, is **11.8 Earth-days**. Any point on the surface experiences approximately 5.9 Earth-days of continuous sunlight followed by 5.9 Earth-days of night.

**Eclipses:**
During each orbit, the moon passes through the gas giant's shadow. For an observer on the sub-jovian hemisphere, this produces a total solar eclipse lasting approximately **4.3 hours**. During this period, the only illumination comes from starlight, lightning, or bioluminescence.

**Tides:**
The gravitational pull of the gas giant raises a substantial tidal bulge in the moon's ocean.

- **Equilibrium Tidal Bulge Height**: The theoretical amplitude of the tidal bulge is approximately **130 meters**. In shallow coastal waters and around the volcanic island's submerged flanks, this manifests as a global tidal range of tens of meters.
- **Tidal Heating Rate**: The combination of synchronous rotation and a slight orbital eccentricity (e ≈ 0.02) generates continuous tidal flexing. The resulting heat flux at the core-mantle boundary is sufficient to maintain the mantle plume driving the super-volcano and prevents complete freezing of the ocean floor. The heating is moderate compared to bodies like Io, ensuring the volcanic island remains structurally stable over geological timescales.

### 7. Magnetic Shielding and Radiation Environment

Thalassa-1b orbits deep within the magnetosphere of its parent gas giant. The gas giant's magnetic field deflects the majority of stellar wind particles and galactic cosmic rays, providing a robust radiation shield for the moon's surface and upper ocean. This shielding is critical for maintaining a stable atmosphere and protecting any potential surface or near-surface biosphere from ionizing radiation.

While the moon itself may possess a weak induced magnetic field from interactions between its salty ocean and the gas giant's magnetosphere, it is the **parent planet's magnetic field** that serves as the primary protective barrier.

### 8. Summary of Habitability and Exploration Potential

Thalassa-1b represents a unique class of ocean world where the interplay of low gravity, tidal heating, and a deep global ocean creates a continuous, accessible liquid water environment from surface to seafloor. The lone volcanic island provides a singular location for surface-based operations and access to mineral resources otherwise sequestered beneath 250 km of water.

Key scientific and exploratory focal points include:
- **Chemosynthetic Ecosystems**: The extensive hydrothermal vent fields on the volcano's flanks are prime candidates for hosting complex life independent of stellar energy.
- **Vertical Connectivity**: The gentle pressure gradient allows for the study of biological and geochemical transport across the entire water column, from the photic zone to the abyssal plain.
- **Exotic Ice Boundary**: The seafloor, poised at the threshold of Ice VI formation, is a natural laboratory for studying high-pressure aqueous chemistry and the transition between liquid and solid phases in planetary interiors.
- **Volcanic Processes**: The volcano itself offers a window into the moon's deep interior, with erupted materials providing samples of the mantle and core composition.
